# PREFACE

## Purpose of This Textbook

This textbook is designed specifically for native speakers of Bahasa Indonesia who are learning English as a second language. It addresses the unique challenges that arise from the structural differences between Bahasa Indonesia and English, particularly in the area of auxiliary verbs[^1] and English tenses[^2].

The material is organized progressively, moving from foundational concepts to more complex structures. Each chapter includes:

- **Conceptual explanations** with clear reasoning
- **Pattern tables** showing sentence structures
- **Comparative analysis** between Bahasa Indonesia and English
- **Error identification** based on documented patterns of Indonesian learners
- **Graduated exercises** from beginner to advanced levels
- **Endnotes** defining technical grammatical terms

## How to Use This Textbook

1. **Read sequentially**: Each chapter builds upon the previous one. Do not skip ahead until you have mastered the current chapter.
2. **Complete all exercises**: Working through the exercises is essential for internalizing the patterns.
3. **Review endnotes**: When you encounter an unfamiliar term, refer to the endnote for its definition.
4. **Practice active production**: After understanding a concept, create your own sentences before moving to the next section.

## A Note on Accuracy

This textbook draws upon established grammatical references and research on English language acquisition by Indonesian speakers. All grammatical rules presented have been verified against authoritative sources[^3]. Where multiple acceptable forms exist, both are presented with explanations of their usage contexts.

# CHAPTER 1: Foundations of English Grammar

## 1.1 What Are Auxiliary Verbs?

An auxiliary verb[^4] (also called a "helping verb" or "kata kerja bantu" in Bahasa Indonesia) is a verb that accompanies the main verb in a sentence to express grammatical tense[^5], mood[^6], or voice[^7]. Unlike Bahasa Indonesia, where verbs do not change form and auxiliary usage is minimal, English requires auxiliary verbs in many common sentence types.

Consider the following comparison:

| Bahasa Indonesia | English (Literal) | English (Natural) |
|---|---|---|
| Dia guru. | He teacher. | He **is** a teacher. |
| Saya tidak suka kopi. | I not like coffee. | I **don't** like coffee. |
| Apakah kamu sudah makan? | Already you eat? | **Have** you eaten yet? |

In each case, the English version requires an auxiliary verb where the Bahasa Indonesia version does not. This is the fundamental structural difference that Indonesian learners must internalize.

## 1.2 The Three Categories of Auxiliary Verbs

English auxiliary verbs can be classified into three distinct categories:

### Category A: Primary Auxiliaries

These verbs can function as both auxiliary verbs and main verbs[^8]:

| Verb | As Auxiliary | As Main Verb |
|---|---|---|
| **be** | She **is** reading. (auxiliary for continuous) | She **is** a teacher. (main verb = identity) |
| **have** | She **has** finished. (auxiliary for perfect) | She **has** a car. (main verb = possession) |
| **do** | **Does** she work here? (auxiliary for questions) | She **does** her homework. (main verb = performs) |

### Category B: Modal Auxiliaries

These verbs express modality[^9] (possibility, necessity, ability, permission) and never function as main verbs:

| Modal | Primary Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **can** | ability or permission | She **can** swim. |
| **could** | past ability or polite request | **Could** you help me? |
| **will** | future or willingness | I **will** help you. |
| **would** | conditional or polite form | I **would** like some tea. |
| **shall** | future (formal) or suggestion | **Shall** we dance? |
| **should** | advice or expectation | You **should** study harder. |
| **may** | permission or possibility | **May** I come in? |
| **might** | slight possibility | It **might** rain. |
| **must** | strong obligation | You **must** wear a seatbelt. |

### Category C: Semi-Modals

These verbs function similarly to modals but have some additional properties[^10]:

| Semi-Modal | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **have to** | external obligation | I **have to** work tomorrow. |
| **need to** | necessity | You **need to** study. |
| **ought to** | moral obligation | You **ought to** apologize. |
| **be going to** | planned future | I **am going to** visit my parents. |

## 1.3 Five Fundamental Rules of Auxiliary Verbs

These rules apply universally to all auxiliary constructions in English:

**Rule 1: Questions require an auxiliary verb.**
You cannot form a yes/no question by simply adding a question mark. An auxiliary must appear before the subject.

| Incorrect | Correct |
|---|---|
| You like coffee? | **Do** you like coffee? |
| She is a teacher? | **Is** she a teacher? |
| He can swim? | **Can** he swim? |

**Rule 2: Negatives require an auxiliary verb plus "not."**
You cannot negate a sentence by simply adding "not" after the subject. An auxiliary is required.

| Incorrect | Correct |
|---|---|
| I not like coffee. | I **don't** like coffee. |
| She not is a teacher. | She **is not** a teacher. |
| He not can swim. | He **cannot** swim. |

**Rule 3: After an auxiliary, the main verb is always in base form[^11].**
This means no -s, -ed, or -ing inflection on the main verb when it follows an auxiliary.

| Incorrect | Correct |
|---|---|
| She doesn't **goes**. | She doesn't **go**. |
| He didn't **went**. | He didn't **go**. |
| They can't **swims**. | They can't **swim**. |

**Rule 4: Two auxiliaries can appear in sequence.**
English allows stacking of auxiliaries in a fixed order: modal + have + be + main verb.

| Structure | Example |
|---|---|
| modal + be + V-ing | She **will be working** tomorrow. |
| modal + have + V3 | She **must have left** already. |
| have + been + V-ing | She **has been waiting** for hours. |
| modal + have + been + V-ing | She **must have been sleeping**. |

**Rule 5: The auxiliary determines the tense.**
The choice of auxiliary signals which tense is being used:

| Auxiliary | Tense Indicated |
|---|---|
| am/is/are | present continuous or passive |
| was/were | past continuous or passive |
| do/does | simple present (questions and negatives only) |
| did | simple past (questions and negatives only) |
| have/has | present perfect |
| had | past perfect |
| will | simple future |
| will + have | future perfect |

# CHAPTER 2: The Verb "To Be"

## 2.1 Overview

The verb "to be" is the most fundamental and versatile verb in English. It serves multiple functions that have no single equivalent in Bahasa Indonesia[^12].

## 2.2 Forms of "To Be"

### Present Tense Forms

| Subject | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I | **am** | I **am** a student. |
| He / She / It | **is** | She **is** a teacher. |
| You / We / They | **are** | They **are** friends. |

### Past Tense Forms

| Subject | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I / He / She / It | **was** | I **was** tired yesterday. |
| You / We / They | **were** | They **were** at home. |

### Non-Finite Forms

| Form | Usage | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **be** (base form) | After modals, in imperatives | You must **be** careful. |
| **been** (past participle) | After have/has/had | She has **been** to Paris. |
| **being** (present participle) | In continuous passives | The house is **being** painted. |

## 2.3 Functions of "To Be"

### Function 1: Copula (Linking Verb)[^13]

"To be" connects the subject to a complement that describes or identifies it:

| Type | Example | Literal Translation | Natural Translation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Identity | She **is** a doctor. | Dia adalah seorang dokter. | Dia seorang dokter. |
| Quality | The food **is** delicious. | Makanannya adalah enak. | Makanannya enak. |
| Location | They **are** at school. | Mereka adalah di sekolah. | Mereka di sekolah. |
| Condition | He **is** sick. | Dia adalah sakit. | Dia sakit. |
| Time | It **is** 3 o'clock. | Itu adalah jam 3. | Sekarang jam 3. |

### Function 2: Auxiliary for Continuous Tenses

"To be" marks ongoing actions:

| Tense | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present continuous | am/is/are + V-ing | She **is** reading a book. |
| Past continuous | was/were + V-ing | They **were** playing soccer. |

### Function 3: Auxiliary for Passive Voice[^14]

"To be" indicates that the subject receives the action:

| Tense | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present simple passive | am/is/are + V3 | English **is** spoken worldwide. |
| Past simple passive | was/were + V3 | The book **was** written in 1950. |
| Present continuous passive | am/is/are + being + V3 | The road **is** being repaired. |
| Past continuous passive | was/were + being + V3 | The car **was** being washed. |

## 2.4 Sentence Patterns with "To Be"

### Pattern 1: Affirmative (Positive) Sentences

**Structure:** Subject + to be + complement

| Tense | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present | S + am/is/are + ... | She **is** a nurse. |
| Past | S + was/were + ... | They **were** happy. |
| With modal | S + modal + be + ... | He will **be** here tomorrow. |

### Pattern 2: Negative Sentences

**Structure:** Subject + to be + not + complement

| Tense | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present | S + am/is/are + not + ... | She **is not** (isn't) a nurse. |
| Past | S + was/were + not + ... | They **were not** (weren't) happy. |
| With modal | S + modal + not + be + ... | He will **not** (won't) be here. |

### Pattern 3: Yes/No Questions

**Structure:** To be + subject + complement?

| Tense | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present | Am/Is/Are + S + ...? | **Is** she a nurse? |
| Past | Was/Were + S + ...? | **Were** they happy? |
| With modal | Modal + S + be + ...? | **Will** he be here? |

**Short Answers:**

| Question | Affirmative | Negative |
|---|---|---|
| Is she a nurse? | Yes, she **is**. | No, she **isn't**. |
| Are they happy? | Yes, they **are**. | No, they **aren't**. |
| Was he at home? | Yes, he **was**. | No, he **wasn't**. |

### Pattern 4: WH-Questions

**Structure:** WH-word + to be + subject + complement?

| WH-Word | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| What | asks about identity/activity | **What is** your name? |
| Where | asks about location | **Where are** you from? |
| When | asks about time | **When was** the meeting? |
| Who | asks about person | **Who is** that man? |
| Why | asks about reason | **Why are** you late? |
| How | asks about manner/condition | **How are** you? |

## 2.5 Common Errors with "To Be"

### Error Type 1: Omission of "To Be"

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She a teacher. | She **is** a teacher. | "To be" is required before a complement. |
| They happy. | They **are** happy. | The copula cannot be omitted in English. |

**Why this error occurs:** Bahasa Indonesia does not require a copula in many sentences. The verb "adalah" is often omitted in spoken Indonesian[^15].

### Error Type 2: Incorrect Selection of Form

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| He are a doctor. | He **is** a doctor. | "He" requires "is," not "are." |
| I is happy. | I **am** happy. | "I" requires "am," not "is." |

**Why this error occurs:** Bahasa Indonesia uses the same verb form regardless of the subject (saya adalah, dia adalah).

### Error Type 3: Using "To Be" for Simple Present

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She is go to school. | She **goes** to school. | Simple present uses do/does, not "to be." |
| I am eat rice. | I **eat** rice. | "To be" is for identity or continuous, not simple present. |

**Why this error occurs:** Learners assume "to be" is required in every sentence, as it appears frequently in translation exercises.

### Error Type 4: Literal Translation of "Adalah"

| Incorrect (Literal) | Correct (Natural) | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| The food is delicious is true. | The food **is** delicious. | English does not require double "to be" structures. |

## 2.6 Literal vs. Natural Translation

Understanding the difference between literal and natural translation is crucial for developing fluency:

| Bahasa Indonesia | Literal Translation (Incorrect) | Natural Translation (Correct) |
|---|---|---|
| Dia adalah seorang guru. | He is a teacher is. | He **is** a teacher. |
| Mereka sedang bermain. | They are playing are. | They **are** playing. |
| Saya ingin menjadi dokter. | I want to become doctor. | I want to **be** a doctor. |
| Kapan Anda lahir? | When you are born? | When **were** you born? |

# CHAPTER 3: The Auxiliary Verbs "Do/Does/Did"

## 3.1 Overview

The auxiliary verbs "do," "does," and "did" are essential for forming questions and negatives in the simple present and simple past tenses. They are the most frequently used auxiliaries in everyday English[^16].

## 3.2 Forms and Usage

| Form | Tense | Used With | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **do** | simple present | I, you, we, they | I **do** not know. |
| **does** | simple present | he, she, it | She **does** not work here. |
| **did** | simple past | all subjects | He **did** not call. |

## 3.3 Functions of "Do/Does/Did"

### Function 1: Forming Yes/No Questions (Simple Present and Past)

**Structure:** Do/Does/Did + subject + base verb?

| Tense | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present (I/you/we/they) | Do + S + verb? | **Do** you like coffee? |
| Present (he/she/it) | Does + S + verb? | **Does** she work here? |
| Past (all subjects) | Did + S + verb? | **Did** he call you? |

**Short Answers:**

| Question | Affirmative | Negative |
|---|---|---|
| Do you like coffee? | Yes, I **do**. | No, I **don't**. |
| Does she work here? | Yes, she **does**. | No, she **doesn't**. |
| Did he call you? | Yes, he **did**. | No, he **didn't**. |

### Function 2: Forming Negative Sentences (Simple Present and Past)

**Structure:** Subject + do/does/did + not + base verb

| Tense | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present (I/you/we/they) | S + don't + verb | I **don't** like coffee. |
| Present (he/she/it) | S + doesn't + verb | She **doesn't** work here. |
| Past (all subjects) | S + didn't + verb | He **didn't** call me. |

**Contractions:**

| Full Form | Contraction |
|---|---|
| do not | don't |
| does not | doesn't |
| did not | didn't |

### Function 3: Emphatic Use

"Do/does/did" can be used to emphasize that something is true, often in response to doubt or denial:

| Neutral | Emphatic | Context |
|---|---|---|
| I **like** coffee. | I **do** like coffee! | When someone says you don't like it. |
| She **speaks** English. | She **does** speak English! | When someone doubts her ability. |
| He **went** to school. | He **did** go to school! | When someone says he didn't. |

## 3.4 Critical Rule: Base Form After Auxiliary

After do/does/did/don't/doesn't/didn't, the main verb is ALWAYS in base form:

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She doesn't **goes**. | She doesn't **go**. | Base form after "doesn't." |
| They don't **eats**. | They don't **eat**. | Base form after "don't." |
| He didn't **went**. | He didn't **go**. | Base form after "didn't." |

**Why this rule matters:** The auxiliary already indicates tense and negation. The main verb does not need additional marking.

## 3.5 Subject-Verb Agreement in Simple Present

In affirmative simple present sentences (without "do/does"), the verb takes -s/-es for third person singular:

| Subject | Verb Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I | base form | I **work** every day. |
| You | base form | You **work** every day. |
| He/She/It | base + -s/-es | She **works** every day. |
| We | base form | We **work** every day. |
| They | base form | They **work** every day. |

## 3.6 WH-Questions with "Do/Does/Did"

**Structure:** WH-word + do/does/did + subject + base verb?

| WH-Word | Example |
|---|---|
| What | **What do** you do for a living? |
| Where | **Where does** she work? |
| When | **When did** they arrive? |
| Who | **Who does** he know here? |
| Why | **Why don't** you like it? |
| How | **How do** you make coffee? |

## 3.7 Common Errors with "Do/Does/Did"

### Error Type 1: Omission in Questions

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| You like coffee? | **Do** you like coffee? | Auxiliary required for yes/no questions. |

### Error Type 2: Using "To Be" Instead of "Do"

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Is she work here? | **Does** she work here? | "Is" is for identity/state; "does" is for actions. |

### Error Type 3: Double Marking

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She doesn't **goes**. | She doesn't **go**. | Base form required after auxiliary. |

### Error Type 4: "Did" for Neutral Past

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| I did go to school yesterday. | I **went** to school yesterday. | "Did + base" is emphatic only; neutral past uses past form directly. |

### Error Type 5: "Not" Without Auxiliary

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| I not went to school. | I **didn't go** to school. | Negative past requires "didn't + base form." |

## 3.8 Literal vs. Natural Translation

| Bahasa Indonesia | Literal (Incorrect) | Natural (Correct) |
|---|---|---|
| Kamu suka kopi? | You like coffee? | **Do** you like coffee? |
| Dia tidak pergi ke sekolah. | She not go to school. | She **doesn't** go to school. |
| Apakah dia sudah makan? | Does she already eat? | **Has** she eaten yet? |
| Saya tidak tahu. | I not know. | I **don't** know. |

# CHAPTER 4: The Auxiliary Verbs "Have/Has/Had"

## 4.1 Overview

The auxiliary verbs "have," "has," and "had" are used to form perfect tenses[^17] in English. They also function as main verbs with meanings including possession, consumption, and experience.

## 4.2 Forms and Usage

| Form | Tense | Used With | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **have** | present | I, you, we, they | I **have** finished. |
| **has** | present | he, she, it | She **has** finished. |
| **had** | past | all subjects | They **had** finished. |

## 4.3 Functions of "Have/Has/Had"

### Function 1: Auxiliary for Perfect Tenses

| Tense | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Present perfect | have/has + V3 | I **have finished** my work. |
| Past perfect | had + V3 | She **had left** before I arrived. |
| Present perfect continuous | have/has + been + V-ing | They **have been waiting** for 2 hours. |
| Past perfect continuous | had + been + V-ing | We **had been running** for an hour. |

### Function 2: Main Verb (Possession)

| Subject | Form | Example |
|---|---|---|
| I/you/we/they | have | I **have** a car. |
| he/she/it | has | She **has** two sisters. |
| past | had | They **had** a big house. |

### Function 3: Main Verb (Consumption)

| Example | Literal Translation | Natural Translation |
|---|---|---|
| I **had** lunch at noon. | Saya memiliki makan siang jam 12. | Saya makan siang jam 12. |
| She **has** coffee every morning. | Dia memiliki kopi setiap pagi. | Dia minum kopi setiap pagi. |

### Function 4: Main Verb (Experience)

| Example | Literal Translation | Natural Translation |
|---|---|---|
| I **had** a great time. | Saya memiliki waktu yang besar. | Saya menghabiskan waktu yang menyenangkan. |
| She **has** a headache. | Dia memiliki sakit kepala. | Dia mengalami sakit kepala. |

## 4.4 Present Perfect: When to Use It

The present perfect is one of the most confusing tenses for Indonesian learners because Bahasa Indonesia has no direct equivalent[^18].

### When to Use Present Perfect

| Situation | Example | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Life experience (no specific time) | I **have been** to Japan. | I have the experience of visiting Japan. |
| Present result of past action | She **has lost** her keys. | She still doesn't have her keys now. |
| Action just completed | They **have just finished**. | The action completed very recently. |
| Duration until now | We **have lived** here for 5 years. | We started living here 5 years ago and still do. |
| With "already/yet/still" | Have you eaten **yet**? | Asking about completion up to now. |

### Present Perfect vs. Simple Past: The Critical Difference

| Present Perfect | Simple Past |
|---|---|
| No specific time mentioned | Specific time mentioned |
| Relevant to the present | Not relevant to the present |
| "I **have visited** Paris." (life experience) | "I **visited** Paris **last year**." (specific time) |
| "She **has lost** her keys." (still doesn't have them) | "She **lost** her keys **yesterday**." (specific time) |

**Key Rule for TOEFL:** If a specific time is mentioned (yesterday, last week, in 2020, when I was young), use simple past, not present perfect.

## 4.5 Past Perfect: When to Use It

The past perfect indicates that one event occurred before another event in the past[^19].

| Situation | Example | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Sequence of events | I **had left** before she arrived. | My departure happened first. |
| Experience before a past time | She **had never seen** snow before moving to Canada. | The experience of not seeing snow preceded the move. |
| Unreal past condition | If I **had known**, I would have helped. | I didn't know (past unreal). |

**Structure:** First event (past perfect) + before/when/after + second event (simple past)

## 4.6 Common Errors with "Have/Has/Had"

### Error Type 1: Omission in Present Perfect

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| I already eat. | I **have** already **eaten**. | Present perfect requires "have/has + V3." |

### Error Type 2: Present Perfect with Specific Time

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| I have visited Paris last year. | I **visited** Paris last year. | Specific time requires simple past. |

### Error Type 3: Incorrect Selection of "Have" vs. "Has"

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She have finished. | She **has** finished. | "She" requires "has" (3rd person singular). |

### Error Type 4: Double Past Participle

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| I have eatened. | I **have eaten**. | "Eaten" is already the past participle; do not add -ed. |

## 4.7 Literal vs. Natural Translation

| Bahasa Indonesia | Literal (Incorrect) | Natural (Correct) |
|---|---|---|
| Saya sudah selesai. | I already finish. | I **have** already **finished**. |

# CHAPTER 5: Modal Verbs

## 5.1 Overview

Modal verbs[^20] express modality -- the speaker's attitude toward the action or state described. They convey meanings such as ability, permission, obligation, possibility, and necessity.

## 5.2 Key Properties of Modal Verbs

Modals have unique characteristics that distinguish them from other auxiliaries:

| Property | Rule | Example |
|---|---|---|
| No inflection | Never add -s, -ed, or -ing | She **can** swim. (not "cans") |
| Base form following | Always followed by base verb | He **can** go. (not "can goes") |
| No double modals | Only one modal per clause | She **can must** go. (incorrect) |
| Inversion for questions | Modal moves before subject | **Can** you help? (not "You can help?") |
| Negation with "not" | Modal + not | She **cannot** swim. |

## 5.3 Complete Modal Verb Reference

### Can / Could

| Modal | Function | Example | Literal | Natural |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| can | present ability | I **can** speak English. | Saya bisa berbahasa Inggris. | Saya bisa berbahasa Inggris. |
| can | informal permission | **Can** I use your pen? | Bolehkah saya menggunakan pulpen Anda? | Pinjam pulpen, boleh? |
| can | informal possibility | It **can** be cold in December. | Bisa saja dingin di Desember. | Kadang dingin di Desember. |
| could | past ability | She **could** run fast when young. | Dia bisa lari cepat saat muda. | Dia bisa lari cepat waktu muda. |
| could | polite request | **Could** I borrow your book? | Bolehkah saya meminjam buku Anda? | Boleh pinjam bukunya? |
| could | future possibility | It **could** rain tomorrow. | Bisa saja hujan besok. | Mungkin hujan besok. |
| could | suggestion | You **could** try calling her. | Anda bisa mencoba meneleponnya. | Coba telepon dia. |

### Will / Would

| Modal | Function | Example | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| will | future prediction | It **will** rain tomorrow. | Simple prediction about the future. |
| will | spontaneous decision | I**'ll** help you. | Decision made at the moment of speaking. |
| will | willingness/offer | **Will** you marry me? | Asking about willingness. |
| will | request | **Will** you open the door? | Polite request. |
| would | conditional (Type 2) | If I had money, I **would** buy a car. | Unreal present condition. |
| would | polite form | **Would** you like some coffee? | More polite than "will." |
| would | past habit | When young, I **would** play every day. | Habitual past action. |
| would | desire | I **would** like to order, please. | Formal way to express wanting. |

### Shall / Should

| Modal | Function | Example | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| shall | formal offer (I/we) | **Shall** I carry your bag? | Formal offer of help. |
| shall | formal future (I/we) | We **shall** overcome. | Formal declaration. |
| should | advice | You **should** study harder. | Recommendation. |
| should | mild obligation | You **should** respect your parents. | Moral expectation. |
| should | expectation | The package **should** arrive today. | What is expected to happen. |

### May / Might

| Modal | Function | Example | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| may | formal permission | **May** I come in? | Formal request for permission. |
| may | possibility (~50%) | He **may** be at home. | It is possible he is at home. |
| may | wish | You **may** live long! | Expressing a hope. |
| might | slight possibility (<50%) | He **might** be late. | Less certain than "may." |
| might | suggestion | You **might** want to check again. | Gentle suggestion. |

### Must

| Modal | Function | Example | Explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| must | strong obligation | You **must** wear a seatbelt. | It is required. |
| must | strong prohibition | You **must not** smoke here. | Smoking is forbidden. |
| must | logical conclusion | She **must** be tired. | I am certain she is tired. |

**Important Distinction:**

| Expression | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| must | internal obligation (strong) | I **must** study. (I feel it is necessary) |
| have to | external obligation | I **have to** wear a uniform. (the rule requires it) |
| don't have to | no obligation | I **don't have to** wear a uniform. (there is no rule) |

## 5.4 Modal + Aspect Combinations

| Modal | + Base Form | + Be (Continuous) | + Have (Perfect) |
|---|---|---|---|
| can | can **go** | can **be going** | can **have gone** |
| could | could **go** | could **be going** | could **have gone** |
| will | will **go** | will **be going** | will **have gone** |
| would | would **go** | would **be going** | would **have gone** |
| should | should **go** | should **be going** | should **have gone** |
| may | may **go** | may **be going** | may **have gone** |
| might | might **go** | might **be going** | might **have gone** |
| must | must **go** | must **be going** | must **have gone** |

## 5.5 Common Errors with Modal Verbs

### Error Type 1: Adding -s to Modals

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She cans swim. | She **can** swim. | Modals never take -s. |

### Error Type 2: Adding "to" After Modal

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| You must to go. | You **must** go. | Modals are followed directly by base form. |

### Error Type 3: Using "Can" for Obligation

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| You can wear a helmet. (intended: obligation) | You **must** wear a helmet. | "Can" indicates ability or permission, not obligation. |

### Error Type 4: Combining Two Modals

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She can must go. | She **must** go. | Only one modal per clause. |

## 5.6 Literal vs. Natural Translation

| Bahasa Indonesia | Literal (Incorrect) | Natural (Correct) |
|---|---|---|

| Anda harus memakai masker. | You must to wear mask. | You **must** wear a mask. |

| Seharusnya Anda datang lebih awal. | You should have came earlier. | You **should have come** earlier. |

# CHAPTER 6: English Tenses and Their Auxiliary Structures

## 6.1 Overview

English has twelve tenses, organized by three time frames (present, past, future) and four aspects[^21] (simple, continuous, perfect, perfect continuous). Each tense has a specific auxiliary structure.

## 6.2 Complete Tense Reference

### Present Tenses

| Tense | Structure | Example | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Simple present | S + V-s/es | She **works** every day. | Habits, general truths |
| Present continuous | S + am/is/are + V-ing | She **is working** now. | Actions happening now |
| Present perfect | S + have/has + V3 | She **has worked** here for 3 years. | Experience, present relevance |
| Present perfect continuous | S + have/has + been + V-ing | She **has been working** since 9 AM. | Duration until now |

### Past Tenses

| Tense | Structure | Example | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Simple past | S + V2 | She **worked** yesterday. | Completed past actions |
| Past continuous | S + was/were + V-ing | She **was working** at 9 AM. | Ongoing past actions |
| Past perfect | S + had + V3 | She **had worked** here before 2020. | Before another past event |
| Past perfect continuous | S + had + been + V-ing | She **had been working** for 2 hours when I arrived. | Duration before another past event |

### Future Tenses

| Tense | Structure | Example | When to Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Simple future | S + will + V | She **will work** tomorrow. | Predictions, plans |
| Future continuous | S + will + be + V-ing | She **will be working** at 10 AM tomorrow. | Ongoing future actions |
| Future perfect | S + will + have + V3 | She **will have worked** here for 10 years by 2030. | Completion before future time |
| Future perfect continuous | S + will + have + been + V-ing | She **will have been working** for 3 hours by noon. | Duration before future time |

## 6.3 Decision Flowchart: Choosing the Correct Tense

```
Is the action happening NOW?
+-- YES --> Is the action ongoing/unfinished?
|           +-- YES --> PRESENT CONTINUOUS (am/is/are + V-ing)
|           +-- NO --> Does it involve experience or duration?
|                       +-- YES --> PRESENT PERFECT (have/has + V3)
|                       |           +-- Is the focus on duration?
|                       |           +-- YES --> PRESENT PERFECT CONTINUOUS
|                       |           +-- NO --> PRESENT PERFECT
|                       +-- NO --> SIMPLE PRESENT (V-s/es)
+-- NO --> Is the action in the PAST?
            +-- YES --> Is there a specific time mentioned?
                        +-- YES --> Is there another past event?
                        |           +-- YES --> PAST PERFECT (had + V3) for the earlier one
                        |           +-- NO --> SIMPLE PAST (V2)
                        +-- NO --> Is the action ongoing/long-duration?
                                    +-- YES --> PAST CONTINUOUS (was/were + V-ing)
                                    +-- NO --> Is it about duration?
                                                +-- YES --> PAST PERFECT CONTINUOUS
                                                +-- NO --> PAST PERFECT
```

## 6.4 Tenses in Context: Same Scenario, Different Tenses

Consider the scenario of someone working:

| Tense | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Simple present | She **works** at a bank. | She has a job at a bank. (permanent) |
| Present continuous | She **is working** right now. | She is at work at this moment. |
| Present perfect | She **has worked** at a bank for 5 years. | She started 5 years ago and still works there. |
| Present perfect continuous | She **has been working** since 8 AM. | She started at 8 and is still working. |
| Simple past | She **worked** at a bank last year. | She no longer works there. |
| Past continuous | She **was working** when I called. | The action was in progress at a specific past time. |
| Past perfect | She **had worked** at a bank before becoming a teacher. | The bank job preceded the teaching job. |
| Past perfect continuous | She **had been working** for 3 hours when her boss arrived. | The working started 3 hours before the boss's arrival. |
| Simple future | She **will work** at a bank next year. | She plans to work there in the future. |
| Future continuous | She **will be working** at 10 AM tomorrow. | The action will be in progress at that time. |
| Future perfect | She **will have worked** there for 10 years by 2030. | The 10-year milestone will be reached by 2030. |
| Future perfect continuous | She **will have been working** for 5 hours by lunchtime. | The duration will be 5 hours at lunchtime. |

## 6.5 Common Errors with Tenses

### Error Type 1: Present Perfect with Specific Time

|---|---|---|

### Error Type 2: Simple Past in Present Perfect Context

### Error Type 3: Missing Auxiliary in Continuous Tense

### Error Type 4: Using "Did" for Neutral Past

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| I did go to school yesterday. | I **went** to school yesterday. | Neutral past uses past form directly. |

## 6.6 Literal vs. Natural Translation

| Bahasa Indonesia | Literal (Incorrect) | Natural (Correct) |
|---|---|---|

# CHAPTER 7: Complex Auxiliary Combinations

## 7.1 Overview

English allows multiple auxiliaries to appear in a single clause. Understanding the correct order and function of these combinations is essential for advanced proficiency.

## 7.2 The Order of Auxiliaries

The fixed order of auxiliaries in English is:

**Subject + (1) Modal + (2) Have + (3) Be + Main Verb**

| Position | Auxiliary Type | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Modal | can, could, will, would, shall, should, may, might, must |
| 2 | Have | have, has, had |
| 3 | Be | be, being, been (+ V-ing for continuous) |
| 4 | Main Verb | base form or -ing |

## 7.3 Examples of Auxiliary Combinations

| Combination | Example | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Modal + base verb | She **can swim**. | Ability in present. |
| Modal + be + V-ing | She **will be swimming** at 3 PM. | Ongoing action in future. |
| Modal + have + V3 | She **must have left**. | Logical conclusion about past. |
| Modal + have + been + V-ing | She **must have been waiting** for hours. | Logical conclusion about past duration. |
| Have + V3 | She **has finished**. | Present perfect. |
| Have + been + V-ing | She **has been waiting** for 2 hours. | Present perfect continuous. |
| Be + V-ing | She **is swimming**. | Present continuous. |

## 7.4 Negation with Multiple Auxiliaries

The word "not" is placed after the FIRST auxiliary:

| Sentence | Placement of "not" |
|---|---|
| She **can not** swim. | After "can" |
| She **will not be** swimming. | After "will" |
| She **has not finished**. | After "has" |
| She **has not been waiting**. | After "has" |

## 7.5 Questions with Multiple Auxiliaries

The FIRST auxiliary moves before the subject:

| Statement | Question |
|---|---|
| She **can** swim. | **Can** she swim? |
| She **will be** swimming. | **Will** she be swimming? |
| She **has** finished. | **Has** she finished? |
| She **has been** waiting. | **Has** she been waiting? |

## 7.6 WH-Questions with Multiple Auxiliaries

**Structure:** WH-word + first auxiliary + subject + remaining auxiliaries + main verb?

| Example |
|---|
| **What** will she be doing at 3 PM? |
| **Where** has he been living? |
| **How long** have they been waiting? |
| **Why** must you always complain? |

## 7.7 Common Errors with Complex Auxiliaries

### Error Type 1: Wrong Order of Auxiliaries

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She has been can waiting. | She has **been** waiting. | Modal + have + be; no two modals. |

### Error Type 2: "Not" in Wrong Position

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She not can swim. | She **cannot** swim. | "Not" goes after the first auxiliary. |
| She can not swim. | She **cannot** swim. | "Not" goes after the first auxiliary. |

### Error Type 3: Auxiliary in Wrong Position in Question

| Incorrect | Correct | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| She can swim? | **Can** she swim? | First auxiliary moves before subject. |

# CHAPTER 8: Common Errors and Remediation Strategies

## 8.1 Overview

This chapter consolidates the most frequent errors made by Indonesian learners of English, drawing upon research in second language acquisition[^22] and error analysis[^23].

## 8.2 Comprehensive Error Table

| No. | Error | Incorrect | Correct | Error Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Auxiliary omission | She a teacher. | She **is** a teacher. | Omission |
| 2 | Double marking | She doesn't goes. | She doesn't **go**. | Verb form |
| 3 | Incorrect "to be" form | He are a doctor. | He **is** a doctor. | Agreement |
| 4 | "To be" for simple present | She is go to school. | She **goes** to school. | Wrong auxiliary |
| 5 | "Do" omission in questions | You like coffee? | **Do** you like coffee? | Omission |
| 6 | "Not" without auxiliary | I not went. | I **didn't** go. | Negative formation |
| 7 | Present perfect with specific time | I have visited last year. | I **visited** last year. | Tense selection |
| 8 | -s on modal | She cans swim. | She **can** swim. | Modal form |
| 9 | "Must to" | You must to go. | You **must** go. | Modal + to |
| 10 | "Can" for obligation | You can wear a helmet. | You **must** wear a helmet. | Modal meaning |
| 11 | Double past participle | I have eatened. | I **have eaten**. | Past participle |
| 12 | Missing "been" in perfect continuous | She has waiting. | She has **been** waiting. | Omission |
| 13 | Article omission | I am student. | I am **a** student. | Article |
| 14 | Uncountable noun article | I drink a coffee. | I drink **coffee**. | Article |
| 15 | Capitalization | I learn english. | I learn **E**nglish. | Capitalization |

## 8.3 Prevention Strategies

### Strategy 1: Pre-Submission Checklist

Before finalizing any sentence, verify:

1. Does the sentence have an auxiliary if needed (negatives, questions, continuous, perfect)?
2. Is the correct auxiliary form used for the subject?
3. Is the main verb in the correct form after the auxiliary?
4. If negative, is "not" placed after the first auxiliary?
5. If a question, is the first auxiliary before the subject?

### Strategy 2: Quick Detection Patterns

| If You Write... | Check For... |
|---|---|
| "is/am/are" | Is this continuous tense or identity? |
| "do/does/did" | Is there -s on the following verb? (There should NOT be.) |
| "have/has/had" | Is there a past participle (V3) following? |
| A modal | Is there "to" or -s/-ed after it? (There should NOT be.) |
| A past negative | Is there "didn't + base form"? |
| A question | Is the first auxiliary before the subject? |

### Strategy 3: Mnemonic for Auxiliary Selection

```
T = To be (am/is/are/was/were) → identity, state, continuous
D = Do/Does/Did → simple present/past questions and negatives
H = Have/Has/Had → perfect tenses
M = Modal (can/will/must/...) → possibility, ability, obligation
```

[^1]: **Auxiliary verb** (kata kerja bantu): Kata kerja yang mendampingi kata kerja utama (main verb) untuk menunjukkan tense, mood, atau voice. Contoh: is, are, have, do, can. Dalam Bahasa Indonesia, penggunaan auxiliary sangat minim dibandingkan Bahasa Inggris.
[^2]: **Tense** (waktu): Bentuk kata kerja yang menunjukkan kapan suatu aksi terjadi -- apakah di masa sekarang (present), masa lampau (past), atau masa depan (future).
[^3]: **Authoritative sources** (sumber otoritatif): Rujukan yang diakui secara akademis sebagai sumber yang dapat dipercaya untuk informasi tata bahasa.
[^4]: **Auxiliary verb** (kata kerja bantu): Kata kerja yang mendampingi kata kerja utama (main verb) untuk menunjukkan tense, mood, atau voice. Contoh: is, are, have, do, can.
[^5]: **Tense** (waktu): Bentuk kata kerja yang menunjukkan kapan suatu aksi terjadi.
[^6]: **Mood** (moda): Bentuk kata kerja yang menunjukkan sikap pembicara terhadap aksi, misalnya kemungkinan (indicative), perintah (imperative), atau kondisi tidak nyata (subjunctive).
[^7]: **Voice** (suara): Bentuk kalimat yang menunjukkan apakah subjek melakukan aksi (active voice) atau menerima aksi (passive voice).
[^8]: **Main verb** (kata kerja utama): Kata kerja yang mengandung makna inti dalam kalimat, berbeda dari auxiliary verb yang hanya berfungsi sebagai pembantu.
[^9]: **Modality** (modalitas): Makna yang menunjukkan sikap pembicara terhadap aksi, seperti kemungkinan (possibility), kewajiban (necessity), kemampuan (ability), atau izin (permission).
[^10]: **Semi-modal**: Kata kerja yang berfungsi seperti modal tetapi memiliki beberapa properti tambahan, seperti bisa diikuti "to" (have to, need to, ought to).
[^11]: **Base form** (bentuk dasar): Bentuk paling dasar dari kata kerja, tanpa infleksi apapun. Contoh: go, eat, write. Digunakan setelah auxiliary verbs.
[^12]: **Copula** (kata kerja penghubung): Kata kerja yang menghubungkan subjek dengan komplemen (deskripsi/status). Dalam Bahasa Inggris, "to be" berfungsi sebagai copula. Dalam Bahasa Indonesia, copula "adalah" sering dihilangkan dalam percakapan.
[^13]: **Complement** (komplemen): Bagian kalimat yang melengkapi makna subjek atau kata kerja. Contoh: "a teacher" dalam "She is a teacher."
[^14]: **Passive voice** (suara pasif): Bentuk kalimat di mana subjek menerima aksi, bukan melakukannya. Contoh: "The book was written by the author."
[^15]: **Copula omission in Bahasa Indonesia**: Dalam Bahasa Indonesia, kata "adalah" sering dihilangkan dalam percakapan sehari-hari. Contoh: "Dia guru" (tanpa "adalah") masih dianggap benar dalam konteks informal.
[^16]: **Frequency of use**: Berdasarkan penelitian korpus bahasa, auxiliary verbs "do/does/did" termasuk yang paling sering digunakan dalam Bahasa Inggris lisan dan tulis.
[^17]: **Perfect tenses** (tenses sempurna): Tenses yang menunjukkan bahwa suatu aksi telah selesai pada waktu tertentu atau memiliki relevansi dengan waktu lain. Menggunakan auxiliary "have/has/had" + past participle.
[^18]: **Present perfect in Bahasa Indonesia**: Bahasa Indonesia tidak memiliki padanan langsung untuk present perfect. Ungkapan seperti "sudah" atau "telah" digunakan, tetapi tidak dengan struktur yang sama dengan Bahasa Inggris.
[^19]: **Past perfect**: Tenses yang menunjukkan bahwa suatu aksi terjadi sebelum aksi lain di masa lampau. Struktur: had + past participle.
[^20]: **Modal verb** (kata kerja modal): Kata kerja bantu yang menunjukkan modalitas -- kemungkinan, kewajiban, kemampuan, izin, atau prediksi. Contoh: can, could, will, would, may, might, must, shall, should.
[^21]: **Aspect** (aspek): Fitur gramatikal yang menunjukkan bagaimana aksi terkait dengan waktu -- apakah sederhana (simple), berlangsung (continuous), selesai (perfect), atau berlangsung dan selesai (perfect continuous).
[^22]: **Second language acquisition (SLA)**: Bidang studi yang mempelajari bagaimana orang mempelajari bahasa kedua, termasuk proses, strategi, dan kesalahan yang umum terjadi.
[^23]: **Error analysis**: Metode dalam linguistik terapan yang menganalisis kesalahan yang dibuat oleh pembelajar bahasa untuk memahami proses akuisisi bahasa mereka.
